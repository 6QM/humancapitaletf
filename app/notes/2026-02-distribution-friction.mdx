<div className="note-prose">

One month into the experiment, the first source of friction did not show up in Core or Growth. It appeared in the distribution layer. The intent on paper was clear: publish short written artifacts and occasional videos that connect the framework to concrete work. In practice, those artifacts kept sliding in the face of urgent tasks.

## The pattern

The pattern was simple. When time pressure increased, distribution was treated as optional. Notes were drafted but not shipped. Video outlines existed but never made it to a recording session. The portfolio view exposed this quietly: Core and Growth allocations were consistently met, while Distribution lagged week after week.

## The adjustment

Rather than interpreting this as a motivation problem, the adjustment was structural. Distribution tasks were moved earlier in the day and explicitly scoped to be smaller and more mechanical. The goal shifted from "publish something interesting" to "publish a trace of what actually happened"—even if that trace is a single clear paragraph or a short clip.

## What changed

This change did not radically increase output, but it reduced the emotional overhead of shipping. Distribution now behaves more like a system check than a performance.

The working assumption is that maintaining a thin but continuous distribution layer is enough to keep the bridge open between day-to-day engineering work, the framework, and any public artifacts that follow.
