After four months of tagging work to buckets, I wanted to do a closer look at what "Core Assets" actually means in practice—not in theory. The framework says Core is about durable skills that create value in any market condition. But that's a definition, not a map.

## What I found

When I listed every project and task tagged as Core over the past 16 weeks, a clearer picture emerged. The bucket contained three distinct types of work:

1. **Deep problem-solving** — Extended sessions on hard engineering problems. These were the clearest Core investments: high focus, specific domain, direct skill accumulation.
2. **Code review and systems thinking** — Reading and evaluating other people's code, architecture decisions, tradeoffs. This was less obvious but clearly Core: it builds judgment, not just execution.
3. **Routine maintenance** — Keeping existing systems running. This consumed roughly 20% of Core time, but it's largely not compounding. It's the cost of existing commitments.

## The insight

Item three is the interesting one. Maintenance work keeps things running but doesn't compound. In a financial portfolio, cash and bonds have a place—but you wouldn't want 20% of your "growth assets" to be cash. The Core bucket needs the same scrutiny.

The question isn't whether maintenance is necessary (it is). The question is whether it belongs in Core's accounting, or whether it should be tracked separately as operational overhead so Core's compounding activity is measured accurately.

## Next adjustment

Starting in Q2, I'm going to track maintenance separately from Core. This doesn't change how I spend time—it changes what the data shows me. If Core's actual compounding allocation is closer to 35% than the apparent 55%, the portfolio looks different than I thought.

That's the point of the audit. Not to optimize, but to see clearly.
