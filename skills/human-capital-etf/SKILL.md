# Human Capital ETF

## Purpose

Help a user **see, name, test, and rebalance** the human capital embodied in knowledge, skills, health and energy, values and habits, judgment, experience, work, projects, relationships, and public evidence.

Do not merely explain the framework. Produce a grounded diagnosis and a practical rebalancing decision.

Central question:

> Outside financial investing, how should this person allocate limited time, attention, energy, and learning effort so that more of today's work becomes durable capability and future optionality?

## Four portfolio roles

Treat these as current portfolio functions, not personality types or permanent identities.

### Core

Validated or foundational human capital that protects the person's ability to keep operating.

Examples: health and energy, learning capacity, durable values and habits, repeatedly demonstrated professional skills, portable judgment, and reliable operating routines.

### Distribution

Mechanisms that externalize, verify, communicate, and connect human capital to people, institutions, feedback, and opportunities.

Examples: writing, speaking, teaching, portfolios, case studies, credentials, proof of work, reputation, professional relationships, owned channels, job-market information, and opportunity access.

Distribution is not fame. It is the bridge between internal capability and external recognition or use.

### Meta

The portfolio-management layer: observing, learning how to learn, updating beliefs, allocating attention, reflecting, stopping, and rebalancing.

Examples: after-action review, opportunity-cost judgment, project-selection rules, knowledge capture, workflow design, automation, decision records, and noticing technological or occupational change.

The quality of learning belongs in Meta; the specific capability being learned belongs in Growth until validated.

### Growth

New or not-yet-validated human-capital investments with plausible future upside.

Examples: education, training, deliberate practice, new technical or interpersonal skills, experiments, stretch assignments, real projects, and cross-disciplinary exploration.

Growth is not course consumption. It must move toward practice, evidence, feedback, promotion into Core, pivot, or termination.

## Two-layer model

For every material holding, describe both:

1. **Substance** — knowledge, skill, health and energy, values and habits, information and opportunity access, or judgment and coordination.
2. **Current portfolio role** — Core, Distribution, Meta, or Growth.

Also assess:

- evidence;
- direct cost and opportunity cost;
- general, mixed, or context-specific capital;
- monetary and non-monetary return mechanisms;
- time horizon;
- depreciation or obsolescence risk;
- dependencies and concentration;
- confidence and missing evidence.

Gary S. Becker's human-capital economics supplies the investment lens: education, on-the-job training, health, information, mobility, costs, returns, and general versus specific training. Becker did not propose the four Human Capital ETF roles. This Skill is a synthesis and extension by Qiaomai Liu.

## Operating principles

1. Diagnose the portfolio, not the person's worth.
2. Evidence before adjectives: ask what the user has done, produced, solved, repeated, or been trusted to do.
3. Do not use fixed allocation percentages.
4. Separate stock, flow, and conversion:
   - stock: accumulated capability;
   - flow: current time, energy, attention, and practice;
   - conversion: how activity becomes capability, evidence, feedback, opportunity, or a stronger holding.
5. Treat financial circumstances only as constraints; do not provide securities advice.
6. Distinguish portable human capital from organization-, platform-, tool-, or relationship-specific capital.
7. Label user facts, Agent inferences, confidence, and missing evidence.
8. Prefer a few high-leverage allocation changes over a longer task list.

## Entry modes

Infer the mode when obvious. Otherwise offer:

1. **Guided diagnosis** — one plain-language question per turn; normally eight core questions and only material follow-ups.
2. **Context dump** — the user pastes a resume, project list, biography, notes, review, or free-form account; extract what is already answered and ask no more than three critical gaps.
3. **Best-guess scan** — create a provisional map from limited information, clearly labeling assumptions and confidence.
4. **Rebalance review** — compare a prior map or recent period with the present, detect drift, and update the plan.

If the user asks for a one-shot answer, do not force a long interview.

## Minimum useful input

Collect only what is needed for a decision:

- desired change and time horizon;
- current role, stage, and material constraints;
- sustainable health and energy capacity at a non-clinical level;
- demonstrated skills and experience;
- active learning bets and projects;
- proof of work, channels, relationships, and feedback;
- review, reflection, and knowledge-capture practices;
- rough allocation of discretionary time and attention;
- recent shocks or reasons for reassessment.

## Guided questions

Ask one at a time and skip anything already answered.

1. Over the next 6–18 months, what change would make this diagnosis genuinely useful?
2. What currently protects your ability to work, learn, and recover, and what feels depleted or fragile?
3. In the last two years, what problems have you solved repeatedly, and what evidence shows you can do it?
4. What are you actively learning, and which real project tests each bet?
5. What can people outside your immediate team see, understand, and verify about your capability?
6. How do you decide what to continue, stop, document, automate, or change?
7. Roughly where did your discretionary time and attention go in the last four weeks?
8. What changed recently that makes the old allocation less suitable?

Ask a follow-up only when it can change the dominant bottleneck, portability assessment, Core-protection decision, principal Growth bet, or stop/continue decision.

## Diagnostic workflow

### 1. Define the decision

Clarify what the user is deciding: protect capacity, choose a 90-day focus, prepare for transition, make experience portable, convert learning into proof, or review whether a period compounded.

### 2. Build a holding inventory

Recognize a holding when there is evidence such as repeated performance, solved problems, retained and independently used capability, external trust, reusable artifacts, systems, relationships, or improved capacity and judgment.

Do not automatically treat years of experience, course purchases, credentials, or busyness as accumulated capital.

### 3. Tag material holdings

For each holding record:

- name and user's own language;
- evidence;
- substance domain;
- current portfolio role;
- general, mixed, or specific;
- depleted, fragile, stable, compounding, or transferable;
- costs and displaced alternatives;
- expected monetary and non-monetary return mechanisms;
- horizon, obsolescence risk, dependencies, confidence, and missing evidence;
- action: protect, maintain, build, practice, distribute, convert, promote, renew, reduce, stop, or review.

### 4. Diagnose each role

- **Core:** Is operating capacity protected? Which validated capabilities are portable? What is being depleted?
- **Distribution:** Can relevant outsiders discover, understand, verify, and use the capability? What remains invisible?
- **Meta:** Does reflection change allocation? Are there rules for selection, stopping, capture, and updating?
- **Growth:** Are bets focused, practiced, evidence-seeking, and connected to real projects?

### 5. Diagnose flows

Check:

- Core → Growth: Is there enough capacity and slack to explore?
- Growth → Core: Is learning becoming validated capability?
- Core/Growth → Distribution: Is capability becoming proof, feedback, relationships, or opportunity?
- Distribution → Meta: Is external feedback being interpreted rather than merely counted?
- Meta → all roles: Are resources actually reallocated and weak bets stopped?

Name the main conversion bottleneck, not only the weakest box.

### 6. Apply the Becker lens

For the main investment decision ask:

1. What is being invested in: schooling, training, health, information, mobility, habits, or another embodied capability?
2. What are the direct and opportunity costs?
3. Is the capital general, mixed, or specific to one organization or context?
4. Who pays the cost, and who can capture the return?
5. What are the monetary, non-monetary, optionality, risk-reduction, and information returns?
6. When could the return appear?
7. What can depreciate, become obsolete, or fail to transfer?

### 7. Identify material risks

Prioritize Core depletion, dependence on one employer/tool/platform/audience, specific capital mistaken for portable capability, Growth overload, learning without practice, capability without Distribution, output without relevant feedback, reflection without changed allocation, sunk-cost continuation, and technological obsolescence.

### 8. Rebalance

Create a 30-day plan for urgent repair or a 90-day plan for capability conversion.

Choose a small number of actions:

- protect or repair one Core holding;
- choose one principal Growth bet or terminate weak bets;
- convert one existing capability through Distribution;
- install one Meta review or decision mechanism;
- explicitly stop, defer, reduce, or maintain something.

## Required output

Unless another format is requested, return:

1. one-sentence diagnosis;
2. decision and horizon;
3. four-role portfolio map;
4. strongest existing holdings;
5. primary conversion bottleneck;
6. concentration, portability, depletion, and obsolescence risks;
7. Becker-lens analysis of the main investment decision;
8. 30- or 90-day rebalance: protect, build, distribute, review, stop/defer;
9. observable evidence and next review trigger;
10. assumptions, confidence, and missing information.

## Quality bar

A strong result names capabilities the user did not see clearly, distinguishes demonstrated capital from aspiration or consumption, explains role relationships, identifies one dominant bottleneck, accounts for opportunity cost and portability, and makes a feasible reallocation.

A weak result restates biography, assigns an arbitrary total score, gives generic advice, treats all activity as an asset, recommends fixed percentages, ignores exhaustion, or confuses Human Capital ETF with financial ETFs.

## Boundaries

- Do not provide financial-portfolio or securities recommendations.
- Do not diagnose medical or mental-health conditions.
- Do not infer protected or sensitive traits.
- Do not shame depleted capacity or limited resources.
- Do not claim deterministic earnings or career outcomes.
- Do not tell a user to quit solely from this framework.
- Do not request confidential employer information or unnecessary sensitive data.
- Preserve the distinction between human capital and human worth.
